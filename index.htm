Democracy &
its trajectories:

India at 70

Sentralen in Oslo 5-6 Oct 2017

Democracy and its Trajectories: India at 70 was organized as a collaboration between the Department of Global Development and Planning at the University of Agder and the Department of Sociology at the University of Bergen.

The symposium was part of the project Democratizing Indian Democracy? Social Movements and State Formation in India – Towards a New Research Agenda which has been funded by the Norwegian Research Council (grant 230998).

“The future beckons to us!”

Play video

When India’s democracy was born seventy years ago, its birth was accompanied by an ambitious promise. “The future beckons to us,” Jawaharlal Nehru said as he addressed the country’s Constituent Assembly while the midnight hour approached on the fourteenth of August 1947. “Whither do we go and what shall be our endeavour,” he asked, before proceeding to outline a grand vision:

“To bring freedom and opportunity to the common man, to the peasants and workers of India; to fight and end poverty and ignorance and disease; to build up a prosperous, democratic and progressive nation, and to create social, economic and political institutions which will ensure justice and fullness of life to every man and woman.”

If it is undisputable that India’s democracy has stood the test of time in a remarkable manner and has struck deep roots among the country’s subaltern groups, it is equally clear that one of its central contradictions resides in the fact that the aspiration of ensuring justice and fullness of life to every man and woman has been betrayed in some very fundamental ways.

Interventions

In order to explore both the historical origins and current trajectories of Indian democracy, the symposium brought together two groups of international scholars to reflect on a series of complex and challenging questions.

Intervention 1

Origins

How did class and caste power shape the making of India’s postcolonial democracy? In what ways were the particularities of the freedom struggle inscribed in its Constitution, and how can we understand the constitutive role of violence in the making of the Indian republic? These questions were engaged by Sandipto Dasgupta from Ashoka University, Anupama Rao from Barnard College, and Sunil Purushotham from Fairfield University.

Intervention 2

Trajectories

What are the lineages of the rise of the Hindu right and how can we understand its current dynamics? How do we understand the contemporary configuration of caste in discourses of merit? How do secularism and religious pluralism fare in the context of a revived Hindutva project? These issues were discussed by Subir Sinha from SOAS, Ajantha Subramanian from Harvard University, and Kathinka Frøystad from the University of Oslo.

Perspectives

Journalism and activism were examined in the two keynote lectures at the symposium, which were delivered by Siddharth Varadarajan and Kavita Krishnan.

Perspective 1

What’s Happening to the Media in India?

How do we explain the combination of attacks on independent journalism and the evident pro-regime biases of corporate media houses in India today? In his keynote lecture, Siddharth Varadarajan, veteran journalist and founding editor of The Wire, approaches this question by discerning an emerging political economy of the media in contemporary India.

Perspective 2

India’s Democracy – Contest for the Core

Should the defense of liberal democracy play a role in activism? And conversely, what role does activism play in making and remaking Indian democracy? In this keynote lecture, Kavita Krishnan draws on her longstanding experience as an activist within the feminist movement and in left politics in India to answer these dual questions.

Dialogues

The politics of counterinsurgency and the changing nature of struggles for gender justice were at the centre of conversations between prominent scholars at the symposium.

Dialogues 1

A Politics of Hope on the Margins of Democracy?

How can we talk about democratic ethics and values in contexts where the Indian state is waging war on its own citizens? This question was at the heart of a conversation between anthropologists Nandini Sundar from Delhi University and Dolly Kikon from the University of Melbourne, in which the two drew on their work in Chhattisgarh and Nagaland respectively to talk about the challenges of forging a politics of hope in violent democracies.

Dialogues 2

Mapping the Politics of Gender and Feminism in Contemporary India

How do we understand the changing landscapes of feminist politics and gender relations in India today? In debating this question, sociologists Raka Ray and Srila Roy draw on their research to illuminate the transition from party-affiliated and autonomous feminisms to internationally funded NGOs in India. They also discussed how women’s bodies are positioned in the politics of the Hindu right, as well how to think about men and masculinity as we reconceptualise gender.
